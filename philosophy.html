02 — Philosophy

Teaching Philosophy

My teaching philosophy has been shaped by over fifteen years of working at the intersection of education, employment, and human development. It has evolved continuously through practice, and most significantly over the course of this program, as I have gained theoretical grounding for what I had long been doing intuitively. At its core, my philosophy is built on the belief that adult learning must be transformative, and that transformative learning is only possible when educators design with deep respect for the whole person.

Core Beliefs About Learning

Before beginning my career, I understood learning primarily as information transfer. If I explained clearly, provided good resources, and broke tasks into manageable steps, I believed learners would succeed. Working in employment services challenged that assumption directly. I watch learners navigate impossible circumstances: writing a résumé while uncertain how they will feed their children, or managing job applications while fearing the loss of already-insufficient social assistance benefits. These experiences taught me that learning is not passive absorption. It happens when individuals actively construct knowledge through meaningful engagement with experience, prior knowledge, and authentic goals. This constructivist understanding is the foundation of everything I do. For learning to be transformative, it requires three conditions: autonomy, safety, and relevance. When individuals are mandated to access employment services through social assistance, their autonomy is compromised before they even walk through the door. The difference in motivation, engagement, and openness between a client who has chosen to seek support and one who has been required to attend is visible from the first interaction. My role as an educator is to create conditions where autonomy, safety, and relevance can coexist, even within systems that work against them.

Role as the Educator

I see myself as a facilitator of autonomous learning rather than a transmitter of knowledge. With a caseload of over 100 individuals and approximately one hour of contact every two to three weeks, traditional instruction is simply not possible in my employment services role. These constraints have been instructive in the best way. Rather than attempting to teach every job search skill, I have learned to prioritize: providing resources, structuring guided self-reflection, and creating space for learners to set intentional goals aligned with their own aspirations. This approach aligns with andragogy and Self-Directed Learning, which emphasize that adults thrive when they have ownership over their educational journey and when learning connects meaningfully to their lives. However, my practice has shown me that self-direction is not equally accessible to everyone. I experienced this clearly when a colleague and I were working with a mother and daughter who were refugees and new to Canada. I noticed the daughter had limited computer skills, a significant barrier to entering the labour market, and suggested referring them to a computer training program. My colleague responded by questioning whether the client was capable of learning at all. That moment crystallized for me how quickly bias can undermine a learner's autonomy, and how critical it is for educators to continuously examine their own assumptions. Transformative learning asks exactly that of us, and I take that responsibility seriously in my own practice.

Technology: Promise and Complexity

Technology occupies a central and genuinely complex place in my philosophy. At its best, it is a bridge to autonomy. Collaborative tools like Google Docs have allowed clients in my employment practice to build and revise their resumes independently between appointments, extending learning beyond our limited contact time. Open educational resources have made it possible to offer quality materials within a near-zero budget. At its worst, however, technology becomes a barrier. Many of my clients' primary device is a smartphone with limited data. Others lack access to Microsoft Word entirely, which means a PDF resume template I send cannot be edited without the right tools. These realities mean that every technology decision I make is also a design decision about who can participate.

This program has sharpened my thinking considerably. I came in understanding technology as a practical tool to be worked around when inaccessible. I leave with a more critical and intentional lens. I now evaluate tools not only for their functionality, but for who they include and who they exclude. When a tool creates feelings of incompetence or shame, it violates the psychological safety necessary for learning. My role is to remain vigilant about these dynamics, and to choose tools that open doors rather than create new barriers.

Equity, Inclusion, and Universal Design for Learning

Equity is not an add-on to my teaching philosophy; it is its foundation. My understanding of Universal Design for Learning has been the most meaningful area of growth for me this semester. I came in knowing that good design should be flexible and accessible. I now understand how to build that flexibility in from the start, through multiple means of representation, multiple pathways to engagement, and multiple options for demonstrating learning.

This has direct implications for my practice. For learners with limited digital literacy, I design printable guides with screenshots alongside mobile-friendly resources. For individuals managing cognitive overload from the stress of poverty or precarious housing, I reduce complexity and prioritize one clear next step. Accessibility, I have come to understand, goes far beyond physical accommodations. It encompasses language, digital access, cognitive load, and the emotional safety required to make mistakes and keep going. Ethical design means normalizing confusion rather than penalizing it, and that principle now shapes every learning experience I create.

Assessment as a Learning Tool

My approach to assessment is rooted in the belief that learning is a process, not a product. In contexts where learners are already under constant external judgment, from dismissive employers to social assistance systems monitoring their compliance, assessment cannot add to that burden. I have seen firsthand what happens when it does: learners disengage, protect themselves, and stop taking risks. What they need instead is feedback that makes their growth visible, celebrates progress even when outcomes are difficult, and builds capacity rather than simply measuring it.

I prioritize a feedback-first model that de-emphasizes outcomes in favour of collaborative evaluation and metacognition. In practice, this looks like helping a client compare their first resume draft to their current version so they can see their own progress clearly. It looks like celebrating the moment a learner confidently articulates a transferable skill for the first time, or independently navigates an online application they would have found overwhelming six months earlier. These process-based achievements frequently matter more for long-term stability and confidence than any single outcome within an arbitrary timeframe.

This philosophy extends to my post-secondary teaching as well. I am known among my students for the time and detail I put into written feedback, and that comes from a genuine belief that every learner deserves to know specifically what they are doing well and exactly where they can grow. Feedback is only useful if it is clear, strengths-based, and actionable, and I hold myself to that standard every time.

My philosophy is grounded in the conviction that adult learning must be transformative, equitable, and designed around the full humanity of the learner. This program has deepened and sharpened that conviction, and it will continue to guide my practice as I move forward.

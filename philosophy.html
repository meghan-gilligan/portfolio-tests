02 — Philosophy

Teaching Philosophy

My teaching philosophy has been shaped by over fifteen years of working at the intersection of education, employment, and human development. It has evolved continuously through practice, and most significantly over the course of this program, as I have gained theoretical grounding for what I had long been doing intuitively. At its core, my philosophy is built on the belief that adult learning must be transformative, and that transformative learning is only possible when educators design with deep respect for the whole person.

Core Beliefs About Learning

Before beginning my career, I understood learning primarily as information transfer. If I explained clearly, provided good resources, and broke tasks into manageable steps, I believed learners would succeed. Working in employment services challenged that assumption directly. I watch learners navigate impossible circumstances: writing a résumé while uncertain how they will feed their children, or managing job applications while fearing the loss of already-insufficient social assistance benefits.

These experiences taught me that learning is not passive absorption. It happens when individuals actively construct knowledge through meaningful engagement with experience, prior knowledge, and authentic goals. This constructivist understanding is the foundation of everything I do. For learning to be transformative, it requires three conditions: autonomy, safety, and relevance. My role as an educator is to create conditions where these can coexist, even within systems that work against them.

Role as the Educator

I see myself as a facilitator of autonomous learning rather than a transmitter of knowledge. With a caseload of over 100 individuals and limited contact time, traditional instruction is simply not possible. These constraints have been instructive; rather than attempting to teach every skill, I prioritize providing resources, structuring guided self-reflection, and creating space for learners to set intentional goals aligned with their own aspirations.

This approach aligns with andragogy and Self-Directed Learning. However, my practice has shown me that self-direction is not equally accessible to everyone. Transformative learning asks us to continuously examine our own assumptions and biases, a responsibility I take seriously to ensure a learner's autonomy is never undermined by institutional or personal prejudice.

Technology: Promise and Complexity

Technology occupies a central and complex place in my philosophy. At its best, it is a bridge to autonomy. Collaborative tools like Google Docs allow clients to build and revise independently, extending learning beyond our appointments. At its worst, technology becomes a barrier for those with limited data or lack of access to specific software. Every technology decision I make is also a design decision about who can participate. I now evaluate tools not only for their functionality, but for who they include and who they exclude.

Equity, Inclusion, and Universal Design for Learning

Equity is the foundation of my teaching philosophy. My understanding of Universal Design for Learning (UDL) allows me to build flexibility in from the start through multiple means of representation, engagement, and expression. Accessibility encompasses language, digital access, cognitive load, and emotional safety. Ethical design means normalizing confusion rather than penalizing it, a principle that shapes every learning experience I create.

Assessment as a Learning Tool

My approach to assessment is rooted in the belief that learning is a process, not a product. I prioritize a feedback-first model that de-emphasizes outcomes in favour of collaborative evaluation and metacognition. Feedback is only useful if it is clear, strengths-based, and actionable. Whether in community practice or post-secondary teaching, I am committed to providing the detail necessary for learners to see their own growth clearly.

This program has sharpened my conviction that adult learning must be transformative, equitable, and designed around the full humanity of the learner. It will continue to guide my practice as I move forward.
